The Last Statements of Texas Death Row Inmates

About the data

Below are different methods of presenting data of executed offenders under the Texas Department of Criminal Justice. The earliest recorded execution from this dataset was on December 7, 1982 and the latest was on October 14, 2015. There are 530 executions total. The ages of offenders executed range from 24 to 67 years of age. The racial categories of those executed are: Black, White, Hispanic, and Other(one offender). As a group, we took the data provided and presented it in various ways. Scroll down to see how the presentation of data changes how we understand it!

The Demographics

The demographics of the inmates are better revealed in the data visualizations. Here the racial discrepancies are depicted in detail. The visualizations show the youngest people to be executed were black (24-25), while those that were the oldest at the time of their death were almost entirely white, with one hispanic inmate aged 54. While the data shows that the number of White inmates made up 44.1 percent of all inmates, their age at time of death is largely in the mid 40s to mid 50s, where as Black inmates who totaled 36.7 percent ranged from mid 30s to mid 40s, and Hispanic (18.6 percent) ranged primarily from late 20s to mid 40s.

We used a word cloud generator to make our word clouds. The first word cloud shows words from all statements, the second is from white inmates, the third is black inmates, and the fourth is hispanic inmates. We can see that from all word clouds the two most common words are: "all" and "love". In the second group is "family" "know" "sorry" "thank" "God". There was no apparent variation in terms of the most common words in their last statements. What we cannot infer from these word clouds is what these common words were used for in the last statements. The use of "all" and "love" can carry varying meanings in different sentences. "God" "Allah" "Lord" "Jesus" "Christ" were all common words in the statements, demonstrating the importance of religion to these inmates. We also cannot visualize the inmates who chose not to give a final statement. These inmates are still included in the demographic information. This brings up issues on how we can visualize silences. As a group we considered using a word that had not been used in the last statements, but that would not have represented the power of these silences.

Our data choices

Since the data set we were visualizing was centred around verbal statements, it made the most sense for us to use word clouds as the focus of our project. We have separated the data into sections based on race and created clouds for each separate group. To complement these clouds, we are also providing demographic information from the dataset in more varied ways, focusing on the relationship between age and race to give viewers an idea of the different people who have ended up on death row in Texas. Many of the inmates chose not to give a final statement. Their data has still been included in the demographic information.

Our design choices

We can read this data differently as a word cloud compared to viewing it in list format of most common words to least common words. The word cloud makes the viewer do more work than simply reviewing a list of words. The word cloud shows the most common words (largest font) amongst some of the less common words (smallest font). This could allow for connections to be made between words (regardless of how common they are) in the statements. In terms of design, for the word clouds we chose to keep the colours and fonts different to see if that affected the way the data is visualized. The different background colours in contrast to the text colour change how much each word stands out from the cloud. From the Black inmates word cloud it is harder to make this differentiation and we rely moreso on the font size. Whereas in the White inmates word cloud the dark green font sticks out the most against the beige background. The words in that font like "family" "Father" "forgive" "pain" would stick out in the word cloud. In contrast to the macabre subject matter of last words, the word clouds elicit a lighter reading. The common words of “love” and “family” are reminiscent of home decor objects, and lose their strength in this context. The tool used (Word It Out) forces whatever subject matter the user gives into this light-hearted reading, skewing the visual data. However, the word cloud remains the easiest way to access and visualize this data. Visualizing the demographic data is innately difficult due to the subject and there is the fear that it reduces the inmates to data points, stripping them of their personhood. Despite this, it does provide valuable insight into the prison system, revealing the racial discrepancies between who is executed and at what age.
